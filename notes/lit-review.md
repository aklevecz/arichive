# Literature Review: Mediated, Hidden Power and the Case for the Personal Platform

*A reading companion for the essay built around the **ariel app** — a single-owner Instagram clone meant as a "philosophical exaggeration" that makes the hidden control of platforms visible — and the broader **membrane / interception** thesis (technology that interfaces with social media so you don't have to, sparing you the ads and the algorithmic feed).*

---

## How to use this document

Seven thinkers, in the order they should appear in your argument. They are **not** a grab-bag. Read in this sequence and they assemble into one continuous claim:

1. **The medium is never neutral** — McLuhan + Latour. The form, and the artifact, shape you regardless of content.
2. **Something is hidden behind the "neutral" thing** — Marx + Debord. The commodity and the spectacle conceal the human relations they're made of.
3. **How that power actually operates, and why it's invisible to its subjects** — a clean chronological spine: Foucault (1975) → Deleuze (1990) → Han (2014). Discipline → continuous modulation → power we exercise on ourselves and call freedom.

Your two artifacts then act *against* this lineage:
- The **ariel app** re-attaches a visible face to a power these thinkers describe as faceless, automatic, and internalized.
- The **membrane / interception** layer is a practical refusal of it — re-writing the script, reclaiming the code, and breaking the mediation.

Each section below has the same shape: **who and the context → the core concepts in depth → how it feeds your thesis → the productive tension → what to actually read first → lines worth quoting.** Where a famous quotation is misattributed in popular usage, I flag it so you don't repeat the error.

## 1. Marshall McLuhan (1911–1980) — *the medium is the message*

### Context
A Canadian professor of English literature who became, almost accidentally, the twentieth century's most famous media theorist. With the economic historian Harold Innis he founded what's now called the **Toronto School of communication theory** — the idea that communication *media* (not just their content) are the decisive force in shaping civilizations. McLuhan's training in literary criticism shows: he thinks in aphorisms, puns, and provocations rather than systematic argument, which makes him quotable and infuriating in equal measure. He was a celebrity in the 1960s (the *Medium is the Massage* LP, the cameo in *Annie Hall*) and then fell out of fashion, only to be rediscovered the moment the internet made his claims feel literal.

### Key works
- *The Mechanical Bride* (1951) — early study of advertising and popular culture.
- *The Gutenberg Galaxy* (1962) — how the printing press created "typographic man" and linear, individualist consciousness.
- *Understanding Media: The Extensions of Man* (1964) — the central book; the one to read.
- *The Medium is the Massage* (1967, with designer Quentin Fiore) — a fast, illustrated distillation; the title's "Massage" is a deliberate pun (the medium *works us over*).
- *Laws of Media: The New Science* (1988, posthumous, with his son Eric) — the "tetrad."

### Core concepts

**"The medium is the message."** McLuhan's foundational claim is that the *content* of any medium is a distraction. What actually restructures human life is the medium itself — the scale, pace, and pattern it introduces into human affairs. The railway didn't matter because of what it carried; it mattered because it created new kinds of cities, work, and leisure regardless of cargo. So when you argue about social media, arguing about the *posts* is a category error. The point is the *feed* — its speed, its endlessness, its architecture. Change the medium and you change everything; leave it intact and better content changes nothing.

**The content is the "juicy piece of meat."** McLuhan's own image: "the content of a medium is like the juicy piece of meat carried by the burglar to distract the watchdog of the mind." The interesting content keeps your attention busy while the medium reprograms you. This is your single most useful McLuhan line, because it explains *why* nobody notices: we are all watching the meat.

**Media as extensions of man.** Every medium extends some human faculty — the wheel extends the foot, print extends the eye, clothing extends the skin — and electric media extend the *central nervous system* itself, putting our perception outside our bodies and wrapping the globe in it.

**Auto-amputation and "Narcissus narcosis."** The companion idea, and the crucial one for your thesis. When we extend a faculty, we also go *numb* to it — McLuhan calls this "auto-amputation." His reading of the Narcissus myth: Narcissus is hypnotized not by self-love but by an *extension of himself* (his reflection) that he fails to recognize as himself. We are all Narcissus before our media, narcotized by our own extensions and therefore unable to perceive their effects. **This is McLuhan's version of "we are not aware of their influence."**

**Environments are invisible.** A late-McLuhan refinement (with the *figure/ground* distinction borrowed from Gestalt psychology): we perceive *figures* (content, events, the post) and ignore the *ground* (the environment, the medium, the platform) in which they sit — yet the ground is where the real action is. "Environments are invisible. Their groundrules, pervasive structure, and overall patterns elude easy perception." A fish, the saying goes, is the last to discover water.

**The global village.** Electric media collapse space and time, "retribalizing" a world that print had individualized — everyone implicated in everyone else's lives, instantly. He meant this with ambivalence, not as utopia: the global village is also a place of total surveillance and tribal friction.

### How it feeds your thesis
McLuhan is the *bedrock* of "platforms are not neutral." The neutrality illusion is precisely the belief that a medium is a passive pipe through which content flows — and that the only thing worth discussing is the content. McLuhan says no: the pipe is the point. Your ariel app is a McLuhan experiment, because it holds the *content* roughly constant (photos, follows, comments, DMs — the Instagram vocabulary) and changes only the *medium* (one owner, no algorithm, no ads). If something shifts in how it feels to use, that shift is by definition a property of the medium, not the posts. And "Narcissus narcosis" + "the juicy meat" together give you a precise mechanism for the invisibility at the heart of your essay: the influence is unfelt because our attention is captured by content and numbed to its own extension.

### Productive tension
McLuhan is a *technological determinist* and proud of it: he tends to treat media as autonomous forces that act on a passive humanity, and he is famously uninterested in economics, ownership, and politics — *who* builds the medium and *who* profits. That's exactly the gap Marx, Latour, and Foucault fill. Use McLuhan for the claim "the medium shapes us"; pivot immediately to the others for "and here is who shapes the medium, and why." Pairing him this way also inoculates you against the common charge that McLuhan lets the powerful off the hook.

### Read first
*Understanding Media*, Chapter 1 ("The Medium is the Message"). If you want the whole argument in an afternoon, read *The Medium is the Massage* cover to cover — it's mostly images.

### Lines worth quoting
- "The medium is the message."
- "The content of a medium is like the juicy piece of meat carried by the burglar to distract the watchdog of the mind."
- "Environments are invisible."
- **Do NOT attribute to McLuhan:** "We shape our tools and thereafter our tools shape us." That sentence was written by **Father John Culkin (1967)**, a friend paraphrasing McLuhan. It's a perfect line — just credit Culkin, or use it unattributed, never as a McLuhan quote.

## 2. Bruno Latour (1947–2022) — artifacts have scripts; technology is society made durable

### Context
A French sociologist and anthropologist of science, the central figure of **Science and Technology Studies (STS)** and the co-founder of **Actor-Network Theory (ANT)**. Latour began as an ethnographer literally sitting in a laboratory watching scientists make facts (*Laboratory Life*), and built from there a sweeping, deliberately strange way of describing how the world holds together. Where McLuhan is aphoristic and grand, Latour is concrete and mischievous: his arguments hang on door-closers, speed bumps, and hotel keys. This is a gift to an essayist — he gives you *objects to point at*.

### Key works
- *Laboratory Life* (1979, with Steve Woolgar).
- *Science in Action* (1987).
- *We Have Never Been Modern* (1991).
- **"Where Are the Missing Masses? The Sociology of a Few Mundane Artifacts" (1992)** — the essay to read; door-closers and seatbelts.
- *Pandora's Hope* (1999) — contains the speed-bump and the "gun" arguments.
- *Reassembling the Social* (2005) — the mature statement of ANT.
- Closely related: **Madeleine Akrich, "The De-scription of Technical Objects" (1992)** — the source of the "script" idea.

### Core concepts

**Actor-Network Theory and the symmetry of humans and nonhumans.** Latour's provocation is that the "social" is not made only of people. It's a *network* of human and nonhuman **actants** — and agency is distributed across the whole network. A traffic system is people *and* lights *and* paint *and* law. To explain how anything stable happens, you have to follow the nonhumans too, and you must treat them *symmetrically* with humans rather than assuming people are the only real actors.

**Scripts and de-scription (with Akrich).** Designers *inscribe* a program of action into an artifact — a **script**, like a film script — that assigns roles and prescribes behavior to whoever uses it. The object "expects" certain users and "tells" them what to do. To analyze a technology is to *de-script* it: read the behavior its design silently demands. A platform's interface is dense with scripts — pull-to-refresh, the infinite feed, the like button, the streak, the "seen" receipt. Each one prescribes a behavior you experience as your own impulse.

**Delegation.** We hand off social and *moral* work to objects. Latour's signature example: the **automatic door-closer** (he calls it the "groom") does the labor of a human who would otherwise have to stand there reminding everyone to shut the door — the machine *disciplines* people on the designer's behalf. The **seatbelt** interlock that won't let you start the car delegates a moral injunction ("be safe") to a mechanism. The **heavy hotel-key fob** that's annoying to carry delegates the request "please return your key" to the weight of brass. Behavior we attribute to our own choices is often a command delegated into matter.

**The speed bump / "sleeping policeman."** In *Pandora's Hope*: engineers translate the moral request "*slow down, for the safety of pedestrians*" into a lump of asphalt that addresses you through a different motive entirely — "*slow down or wreck your suspension*." The political/ethical command is **translated** into the physical world and changes form along the way. The driver obeys morality without feeling moral. This is the cleanest possible image of how a platform's values reach you re-coded as "features."

**"Technology is society made durable."** (The title of a 1990 essay.) Power relations are fragile when they live only in words and habits; they become *durable* — they last, they scale, they enforce themselves — when they're built into material things. Software is the purest case: an executive's preference, once shipped, acts on hundreds of millions of people, around the clock, without anyone re-deciding it. The decision is frozen into the artifact.

**Black-boxing.** Once a network is stable and working, it becomes a **black box**: we stop asking how it works or who assembled it, and just use the output. A black box is, almost by definition, the thing whose politics we no longer see — until it breaks.

### How it feeds your thesis
If McLuhan gives you "the medium is not neutral," Latour gives you the *mechanism*, and it's far more precise and more damning. The platform is not a neutral space; it is a dense bundle of **scripts** that **delegate** its owners' interests into your behavior, **made durable** in code, and then **black-boxed** so you stop seeing the hand that wrote it. "We are not aware of their influence" is, in Latour's terms, the black-boxing of a script. Two payoffs for your project specifically:
- The **ariel app opens the black box.** By being obviously, personally owned, it un-blackboxes the platform — you can no longer pretend "this is just how feeds work," because here is the person who decided how this one works.
- The **membrane / interception layer is re-inscription.** When you build software that pulls only what you asked for and renders it your way, you are stripping out the platform's script and writing your own into the artifact. You take back authorship of the prescription.

### Productive tension
ANT's insistence on *symmetry* — treating all actants alike — has been criticized for **flattening power**: if a billionaire's app and a single user's phone are both just "actants in a network," you can lose sight of the gross asymmetry between them. Don't let Latour talk you out of naming who has more power. Use him for the granular *how* (scripts, delegation, durability); let Marx and Foucault restore the *asymmetry* and the *interests*.

### Read first
"Where Are the Missing Masses?" (essay, ~25 pages) is the perfect entry. Pair it with Akrich's "The De-scription of Technical Objects" for the script vocabulary. The speed-bump passage in *Pandora's Hope* is a couple of pages and worth finding for the quote.

### Lines worth quoting
- "Technology is society made durable."
- The speed bump as a "sleeping policeman": the translation of *slow down for pedestrians* into *slow down for your suspension*.
- The door-closer / "groom" delegating human discipline to a machine.

## 3. Karl Marx (1818–1883) — commodity fetishism and the hidden human relation

### Context
You don't need all of Marx for this essay, and a full survey would bury the one idea that does the heavy lifting. The relevant Marx is the analyst of **how capitalism makes its own workings invisible** — specifically the theory of **commodity fetishism**, supported by his accounts of **alienation** and **ideology**. Keep the section tight and focused on concealment, because concealment is your theme.

### Key works (the relevant ones)
- *Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844* — the theory of **alienation**.
- *The German Ideology* (1846, with Engels) — **ideology** and the "ruling ideas."
- *Capital*, Volume 1 (1867), Chapter 1, Section 4, "The Fetishism of Commodities and the Secret Thereof" — the **core text** for you, and short.

### Core concepts

**Commodity fetishism.** This is the keystone. Under capitalism, Marx argues, the **social relations between people** — the human labor, cooperation, and dependence that actually produce things — come to *appear* as **relations between things**, and as natural properties of the commodities themselves. A price seems to be an intrinsic quality of an object, like its weight, rather than the congealed trace of human labor and a whole web of social relations. In his words, "a definite social relation between men… assumes, in their eyes, the fantastic form of a relation between things." We bow to the object and forget the people. He calls it a *fetish* by analogy with religious objects that worshippers endow with a power that in fact came from the worshippers themselves.

**Alienation.** From the 1844 Manuscripts: under capitalism the worker is estranged on four fronts — from the *product* of their labor (it belongs to another), from the *act* of laboring (it's coerced, not expressive), from their *species-being* (their human capacity for free, creative activity), and from *other people*. The thing you make stands over against you as an alien power.

**Ideology and the "ruling ideas."** From *The German Ideology*: "The ideas of the ruling class are in every epoch the ruling ideas." A dominant order doesn't only control production; it controls the production of *ideas*, so that arrangements that serve the powerful come to seem like plain common sense, neutral, natural, the-way-things-are. Ideology, in his famous image, works like a *camera obscura*, presenting reality upside-down.

**The digital extension (Marxist media theory).** Two later moves are worth knowing because they apply Marx directly to your subject. **Dallas Smythe's "audience commodity" (1977):** in commercial media, the real product being sold is *you* — your attention is packaged and sold to advertisers; the "content" is just bait to assemble the audience. And contemporary **digital-labor** theory (e.g. Christian Fuchs): on platforms, users perform unwaged *labor* — posting, liking, tagging — that produces the data and engagement from which the owners extract value. You work for the platform for free and experience it as leisure.

### How it feeds your thesis
Commodity fetishism is, almost word for word, the engine of "we are not aware of their influence." The feed presents itself as a *thing* with natural properties — "this is just how the timeline behaves" — and that thing-like appearance **conceals the human relations behind it**: the engineers, the executives, the advertisers, the decisions, the extraction of your attention as value. The platform is a fetish object in the strict Marxian sense. Your ariel app is an act of **de-fetishization**: it forcibly restores the hidden human relation to view — *this is not a neutral thing, this is my app, I made these choices, the relation behind the screen is a relation to me, Ariel.* The "ruling ideas" point gives you the language for platform neutrality itself: the belief that platforms are neutral conduits is the *ideology* of the platform owners, circulating as common sense. And the audience-commodity / digital-labor strand sharpens the single-owner provocation into an unavoidable question: on the big platforms, *who profits from your activity?* — a question the ariel app makes impossible to dodge.

### Productive tension
Marx reduces almost everything to economics and class. The thinkers who follow (Foucault especially, and Han) argue that power and control operate through *more* than ownership and money — through knowledge, norms, the psyche — and that the modern subject is disciplined and self-exploiting in ways pure political economy can't capture. So use Marx for the *concealment* and the *extraction*; let Foucault and Han carry the parts of control that aren't about money at all.

### Read first
*Capital* Vol. 1, Ch. 1, §4 ("The Fetishism of Commodities") — only a few pages and astonishingly relevant. Then the short "ruling ideas" passage from *The German Ideology*.

### Lines worth quoting
- "A definite social relation between men… assumes, in their eyes, the fantastic form of a relation between things." (commodity fetishism)
- "The ideas of the ruling class are in every epoch the ruling ideas." (*The German Ideology*)

## 4. Guy Debord (1931–1994) — the spectacle is a social relation mediated by images

### Context
A French Marxist, filmmaker, and the leading figure of the **Situationist International** (1957–1972), an avant-garde political-artistic group. Debord extends Marx into the era of mass media and consumer abundance: if Marx's nineteenth century was about the commodity in the *factory*, Debord's twentieth is about the commodity having colonized *all of lived experience* and turned it into a show. He writes in numbered **theses** — short, dense, aphoristic paragraphs — which makes him very quotable and a little oracular. He's also the bridge from "diagnosis" to "tactics," because the Situationists cared about how to *fight back*.

### Key works
- ***The Society of the Spectacle* (1967)** — 221 numbered theses; the core text.
- *Comments on the Society of the Spectacle* (1988) — a chilling, predictive update.
- His films, and the Situationist concepts of *détournement*, *dérive*, and *psychogeography*.

### Core concepts

**The spectacle is not images — it's a social relation.** The single most important correction Debord makes, and the line to build on. **Thesis 4:** "The spectacle is not a collection of images; rather, it is a social relationship between people that is mediated by images." The spectacle is not "the media" or "advertising" as a thing you could switch off; it's a *condition* in which our relationships to each other run *through* representations rather than direct experience.

**Life as accumulated spectacle.** **Thesis 1:** "In societies where modern conditions of production prevail, all of life presents itself as an immense accumulation of spectacles. Everything that was directly lived has receded into a representation." (Note the deliberate echo of the opening of *Capital*, where capitalist society presents itself as "an immense accumulation of commodities.")

**The spectacle as capital become image.** **Thesis 34:** "The spectacle is capital accumulated to the point that it becomes images." This is the hinge from Marx: fetishized capital doesn't just hide behind commodities anymore, it *appears* directly as the imagery that saturates life.

**Separation and passivity.** The spectacle *separates* — it isolates people from one another and from their own lives, and it induces *passivity*: you watch rather than act, contemplate rather than live. The spectator's bond is one-way; the more they look, the less they live.

**Three forms of spectacle.** Debord distinguishes the *concentrated* spectacle (the cult of a dictator, totalitarian states) and the *diffuse* spectacle (consumer capitalism, advertising, the West). In 1988 he added a third, the **integrated spectacle** — the fusion of the two, in which spectacular power has woven itself into every part of reality, surveillance is total, and nobody can stand outside it. Written before the web, it reads now as a description of the platform era.

**Détournement and the dérive (the tactics).** *Détournement* is the **hijacking** and repurposing of existing media and artifacts against their intended meaning — turning the system's own materials into a critique of it. The *dérive* ("drift") is an unplanned journey through the city that breaks habitual, managed experience. These matter because they are *methods of resistance*, not just diagnosis.

### How it feeds your thesis
Debord wrote the diagnosis of social media decades before it existed. On Instagram, relationships are *literally* mediated by images: your bond with a friend runs through their grid, their stories, their highlights — Thesis 4 made concrete. The *integrated spectacle* of 1988 is an uncanny prediction of the platform you're critiquing — total, ambient, surveilled, inescapable. And the Situationist *tactics* give a name and a lineage to your **membrane / interception** strategy: building software that repurposes the platform's own infrastructure against its intended use is a form of **détournement**. The ariel app, too, is détournement — it hijacks the entire grammar of Instagram (the feed, the follow, the like) and turns it into a critique of Instagram. Debord also supplies the *stakes*: the spectacle produces *separation and passivity*, and your project's implicit promise is the opposite — to restore something "directly lived," to make people act (build, own, connect) rather than scroll.

### Productive tension
Debord is *totalizing* and bleak — if the spectacle is everywhere and inescapable, how does anyone resist? And the Situationist remedies (détournement, drifting) can look romantic and small against a force described as all-encompassing. But that very romanticism is *useful* to you: your essay is, at bottom, a hopeful, build-your-own-tools romanticism, and Debord gives it a serious genealogy. Just be honest that the spectacle absorbs most attempts to oppose it — which is precisely why the *exaggeration* of the ariel app, rather than another earnest "better feed," might be the move that can't be absorbed.

### Read first
*The Society of the Spectacle* — read theses 1–34 first; that's the conceptual core, and it's aphoristic so you can read in short bursts. Then dip into *Comments on the Society of the Spectacle* (1988) for the eerily predictive "integrated spectacle."

### Lines worth quoting
- "The spectacle is not a collection of images; rather, it is a social relationship between people that is mediated by images." (Thesis 4)
- "Everything that was directly lived has receded into a representation." (Thesis 1)
- "The spectacle is capital accumulated to the point that it becomes images." (Thesis 34)

## 5. Michel Foucault (1926–1984) — discipline, the Panopticon, and power without an owner

### Context
A French philosopher and historian, probably the most influential theorist of **power** in the last century. Foucault's lifelong project was to show that power is not mainly a thing held by a king or a state and wielded from the top down, but something **diffuse, productive, and relational** that runs through institutions, knowledge, and everyday practices — and that it *makes* the kinds of subjects we are. For your essay, the crucial book is *Discipline and Punish*, and within it the chapter on the Panopticon.

### Key works
- *Madness and Civilization* (1961), *The Birth of the Clinic* (1963), *The Order of Things* (1966), *The Archaeology of Knowledge* (1969).
- ***Discipline and Punish: The Birth of the Prison* (1975)** — the core text; read the "Panopticism" chapter.
- *The History of Sexuality*, Vol. 1 (1976) — power as productive, "power is everywhere," biopower.
- The Collège de France lectures, especially *The Birth of Biopolitics* (1978–79) — **governmentality** and the neoliberal "entrepreneur of the self," which is the direct bridge to Han.

### Core concepts

**Power/knowledge.** Foucault hyphenates them deliberately: power and knowledge are not opposed (knowledge is not a neutral refuge from power); they imply one another. Every form of knowledge — statistics, medicine, psychology, the data profile — is bound up with a way of managing people, and every regime of power produces its own knowledge. To know is, in part, to control.

**From sovereign power to disciplinary power.** *Discipline and Punish* opens with a gruesome public execution and then a dull prison timetable, decades apart, to dramatize a shift. **Sovereign power** was spectacular, occasional, and aimed at the body (the scaffold). **Disciplinary power**, the modern form, is continuous, quiet, and aimed at *training* — it produces "docile bodies" through surveillance, timetables, drills, ranking, and the **examination**. It operates in **enclosures**: the prison, the school, the barracks, the factory, the hospital. And it is **productive**, not merely repressive — it doesn't just forbid, it *manufactures* habits, norms, and selves.

**The Panopticon.** Foucault takes Jeremy Bentham's 1791 prison design as the diagram of modern power. A ring of backlit cells surrounds a central tower; the guard in the tower can see into every cell, but the inmates can never tell whether they're being watched at any given moment. The effect: each inmate, *assuming* they might be observed, begins to **watch themselves** and behave — they internalize the gaze. Foucault's key formulations: visibility becomes "a trap"; the design "induces in the inmate a state of conscious and permanent visibility that assures the automatic functioning of power." Two consequences are decisive for you. First, power becomes **automatic** — it no longer depends on a guard actually being there. Second, it becomes **disindividualized** — "it does not matter who exercises power"; the machine works no matter who, if anyone, is in the tower.

**Power is diffuse and relational.** From *The History of Sexuality* Vol. 1: "Power is everywhere; not because it embraces everything, but because it comes from everywhere." Power is not a commodity one *possesses* and stores up; it is *exercised* from innumerable points, through relations. There is no single throne.

**Governmentality and the entrepreneur of the self.** In his late lectures Foucault analyzes **neoliberalism** as a *rationality* that reshapes the subject into a little enterprise — *homo economicus* as an "entrepreneur of himself," who manages life as a portfolio of human capital. This is the seed that Byung-Chul Han grows into a whole diagnosis.

### How it feeds your thesis
Foucault names the deep structure of the platform. The feed is a **digital Panopticon**: you exist in a state of *permanent potential visibility* (your activity, your data, your "seen" status, the possibility that anyone or any algorithm is watching), and so you **discipline yourself** — you curate, you perform, you self-edit, because you *might* be seen. Crucially, his "**automatic functioning of power**" and his "**it does not matter who exercises power**" diagnose *why the controller is invisible*: the system is built to run on its own, the tower can appear empty, the power feels ownerless and natural. This is the most rigorous version of "we are not aware of their influence."

**And here is your sharpest single move.** Foucault insists modern power has *no single owner* — that's the whole point of the disindividualized Panopticon. The big platforms *exploit exactly this*: they present their power as automatic, ambient, ownerless ("it's the algorithm," "it's just the platform") in order to hide the fact that there is, in cold fact, an owner — a person, a board, a set of interests. The **ariel app reverses two centuries of power becoming faceless**: it collapses the empty-tower Panopticon back into an old-fashioned *sovereign*, and puts one obvious, named human — you — visibly in the tower. It re-personalizes a power the platforms worked hard to depersonalize. That is a genuine, citable intellectual contribution your essay can claim: not "platforms are panopticons" (everyone says that), but "platforms weaponize the *ownerlessness* of the panopticon, and the cure is to make ownership visible again."

### Productive tension
The diffuse-power model is so good at explaining how power is *everywhere* that it can make power seem to belong to *no one* — which is convenient for actual billionaires. Critics (including Marxists) charge that Foucault dissolves responsibility. **For you this isn't a weakness to apologize for — it's the engine of your argument**, because your whole point is that the platforms *trade on* this dissolution, and your artifacts re-locate the responsibility. Name the tension explicitly and you turn a standard critique of Foucault into the pivot of your essay.

### Read first
*Discipline and Punish*, Part Three, Chapter 3, "Panopticism." If you read one chapter of theory for this essay, read that one. Follow with the "Method" pages on power in *The History of Sexuality* Vol. 1 for the "power is everywhere" passage.

### Lines worth quoting
- "Visibility is a trap."
- The Panopticon "induces in the inmate a state of conscious and permanent visibility that assures the automatic functioning of power."
- "It is not necessary [that] he [be] actually [watched]… he should be sure that he may always be so." (paraphrase the self-policing mechanism)
- "Power is everywhere; not because it embraces everything, but because it comes from everywhere."

## 6. Gilles Deleuze (1925–1995) — societies of control and the algorithmic feed, predicted

### Context
A major French philosopher with a vast, difficult body of work (much of it co-written with Félix Guattari: *Anti-Oedipus*, *A Thousand Plateaus*). For *your* essay you need only **one short, freestanding text**, and you should resist the temptation to drag in the rest — it would dilute a perfect, surgical fit. That text is the **"Postscript on the Societies of Control,"** an essay of roughly five pages that updates Foucault for the digital age and, written in 1990, reads as if it were describing your phone.

### Key work
- **"Postscript on the Societies of Control"** (written 1990; published in English in *October* vol. 59, 1992). Five pages. Read the whole thing.

### Core concepts

**From enclosures to control.** Deleuze begins by accepting Foucault's "disciplinary societies" — the world of **enclosures**, the discrete institutions (family, school, barracks, factory, prison) you pass *through* in sequence, each a fresh start with its own walls. But, he argues, those enclosures are everywhere "in crisis": reform after reform tries to fix the school, the prison, the hospital, but they're being replaced by something new. We are entering **societies of control**.

**Molds versus modulation.** The key distinction. Disciplinary enclosures are **molds** — fixed castings you're shaped in, one at a time. Control is **modulation** — and here is the sentence to quote: control is "a self-deforming cast that will continuously change from one moment to the other, or like a sieve whose mesh will transpose from point to point." Control has no walls and no end. You are never *done*; there is no graduation, only continuous adjustment. As he puts it, in control societies "one is never finished with anything."

**The individual becomes the "dividual."** In disciplinary societies you were an *individual* within a *mass* (a signature and a number, a citizen in a population). In control societies, "individuals have become 'dividuals,'" and masses have become "samples, data, markets, or 'banks.'" You are *divided up* — sliced into data points, scores, and profile fragments that can be recombined and addressed without the whole person ever being in the room.

**Codes and passwords.** Disciplinary order ran on *watchwords* (the order, the slogan). Control runs on **codes**: "the numerical language of control is made of codes that mark access to information, or reject it." What governs you now is what you can log into, what the system grants or denies — passwords, access, the gate that silently opens or doesn't.

**"The corporation is a spirit, a gas."** Where the factory was a body that fixed wages and concentrated workers, the corporation operates by perpetual, modulating *competition* — bonuses, rankings, continuous assessment — setting individuals against one another in a contest that never ends. The image: the factory is solid, the corporation is "a spirit, a gas."

### How it feeds your thesis
This is the **best description of the algorithmic feed ever written, and it predates the feed by fifteen-plus years.** Run the mapping and it's almost embarrassing:
- **Modulation / "self-deforming cast":** the feed is never the same twice; it continuously reshapes itself to you in real time. There is no end to it — infinite scroll is *modulation made literal*. "One is never finished with anything" = you can never reach the bottom.
- **The dividual:** the platform does not address *you*; it addresses your *profile* — a recombined bundle of data points, ad segments, and engagement scores. You experience a feed assembled for a statistical ghost wearing your name.
- **Codes and passwords:** your entire relationship to the platform is gated by access — the login, the personalized, permissioned, password-shaped experience. What you can see is what the code grants.

Where Foucault's Panopticon is still **architectural** (an enclosure, a building, a place), Deleuze captures what's genuinely new: control that is **spaceless, continuous, and ambient**, with no walls to stand outside of. That's the feed exactly.

And it sharpens *both* of your artifacts:
- The **membrane / interception** layer is a **refusal of modulation**. To pull only what you chose, in a fixed order, with an end, is to deliberately re-introduce a *boundary* — to re-build an enclosure, on your terms, against a power whose whole nature is to have none. It is also a reclaiming of the **code**: *you* hold the password now; *you* decide what is granted and what is rejected.
- The **ariel app makes you an individual again, not a dividual.** It refuses to slice you into a data-profile to be modulated; it addresses whole people who chose to follow a whole person.

### Productive tension
The "Postscript" is *suggestive, not systematic* — it's a five-page sketch, explicitly a "postscript," and Deleuze doesn't work the mechanics out. But for an essayist that brevity is a feature: it's quotable, self-contained, and you're not on the hook for the whole Deleuzian metaphysics. Cite it for the *image* (modulation, the dividual, the gas) and don't pretend it's a complete theory.

### Read first
The "Postscript on the Societies of Control" in full. It is genuinely about five pages; there's no excuse not to read all of it, and it will reshape how you see your phone.

### Lines worth quoting
- Control is "a self-deforming cast that will continuously change from one moment to the other."
- "Individuals have become 'dividuals,' and masses [have become] samples, data, markets, or 'banks.'"
- "The numerical language of control is made of codes that mark access to information, or reject it."
- "One is never finished with anything." / "The corporation is a spirit, a gas."

## 7. Byung-Chul Han (b. 1959) — psychopolitics, transparency, and power that feels like freedom

### Context
A Korean-born German philosopher who has become the most-read cultural critic of the digital condition. Han writes very short, aphoristic books (often under 100 pages) that condense and update the whole tradition above — he is explicitly building on Foucault and Deleuze — for the era of the smartphone, the platform, and the self-optimizing subject. He is the **capstone** of your lineage because he answers the question the others leave hanging: *if power is this pervasive, why don't we feel oppressed, and why don't we resist?* His answer: because today's power doesn't oppress — it **pleases**, and we do it to ourselves.

### Key works
- *The Burnout Society* (2010 / Eng. 2015) — the achievement-subject.
- *The Transparency Society* (2012 / Eng. 2015) — compulsory exposure.
- *In the Swarm: Digital Prospects* (2013 / Eng. 2017) — the digital "swarm" and the loss of respect/distance.
- ***Psychopolitics: Neoliberalism and New Technologies of Power* (2014 / Eng. 2017)** — the central text for you.
- Later: *The Disappearance of Rituals*, *Non-things*, *Infocracy* (2021) — the regime of information.

### Core concepts

**From the obedience-subject to the achievement-subject.** In *The Burnout Society*, Han argues we've left Foucault's disciplinary world of prohibition ("you *should not*") for a world of positivity and possibility ("you *can*," "Yes, we can"). The modern **achievement-subject** is not commanded by an external master; it is its *own* master — and therefore its own slave. It exploits itself voluntarily, in the name of freedom and self-realization, until it collapses into burnout and depression. "The achievement-subject is perpetrator and victim at once."

**Psychopolitics and "smart power."** This is the central idea. Foucault's *biopolitics* disciplined *bodies* in enclosures. Neoliberal **psychopolitics** governs the *psyche* — and it does so not by repression but by *seduction*. Han calls it **"smart power"**: power that "adapts itself to the psyche… instead of disciplining it and subjecting it to commands and prohibitions"; power that "reads and pleases" rather than forbidding. It works by making you *want* what it wants. It is friendly, permissive, "like," emoji — and far more total for it, because there is nothing to push against.

**Freedom as the form of coercion.** Han's most arresting claim: under smart power, **freedom itself becomes the instrument of domination.** "Today we do not deem ourselves subjugated subjects, but rather projects: always refashioning and reinventing ourselves." We are not forced to post, to optimize, to expose — we *choose* to, freely, and it is precisely through that free choice that we are governed. The exploitation feels like self-expression; that's what makes it inescapable. "The feeling of freedom… is the very source of the new mode of subjugation."

**The transparency society.** In *The Transparency Society*, Han attacks transparency not as an innocent good but as a *coercive* imperative of the control regime: everything must be exposed, displayed, made into information; negativity, secrecy, distance, and mystery are eliminated. We are compelled to turn ourselves inside out, and we *enjoy* doing it. Crucially, he updates the Panopticon: the **digital panopticon** has no central tower and no external guard, because **the inmates build it themselves and illuminate themselves voluntarily** — we expose ourselves and each other, and call it connection.

**The swarm.** In *In the Swarm*, Han distinguishes the old *crowd/mass* (which had a soul, a direction, a "we" capable of collective action) from the digital **swarm** — a scattered set of isolated individuals with no shared self, no "we," prone to the fleeting fury of the *shitstorm* but incapable of solidarity. Digital communication erodes *respect* (which requires distance) and *the gaze of the other*.

**The smartphone as devotional object.** A vivid image worth knowing: Han likens the smartphone to a **digital devotional object**, the "Like" to the religious "Amen," the practice of self-tracking to a confession. We pray to it; we account for ourselves to it.

### How it feeds your thesis
Han is where "we are not aware of their influence" reaches its purest and most disturbing form. The others explain how influence is *hidden*; Han explains why **we don't even experience it as influence at all** — we experience it as our own free, joyful self-expression. The platform never forces you; it *pleases* you, and your compliance feels like autonomy. This is the deepest possible version of the invisibility at the heart of your essay, and it's the natural climax of the lineage: Foucault's power is exercised *on* us, Deleuze's modulates *around* us, and Han's is the power we lovingly exercise *on ourselves*.

It also does two specific things for your artifacts:
- It **sharpens the ariel-app provocation into something morally pointed.** By making the controller *external, named, and visible* — obviously *Ariel's* app — you puncture the central illusion Han diagnoses: that your activity is purely your own free self-expression. You *re-externalize* a power that smart power had hidden by internalizing. The app says: someone other than you set the terms here — which, on every platform, is also true, and which smart power exists to make you forget.
- It gives the **membrane / interception thesis** its moral content. To intercept the feed, strip the ads, and refuse the algorithm is, in Han's terms, a **refusal of compulsory transparency** — re-introducing *negativity*, *distance*, *the right not to be seen and not to see*. It reclaims the "no" in a regime built entirely on a frictionless "yes."

**One honest discomfort to put in the essay.** Han's *transparency* critique also cuts at your own design. A single-broadcaster app, where one person exhibits themselves to followers, is in one light the very *society of exhibition* he condemns — pure self-display. Don't hide from this; *use* it. The exaggeration works *because* it's a little uncomfortable: you make yourself the visible exhibitionist-sovereign so that the invisible ones become legible by contrast. Naming the discomfort is what turns the app from a gadget into an argument.

### Productive tension
Han is sweeping, aphoristic, and deliberately pessimistic; critics fault him for thin empirical grounding, for romanticizing a pre-digital past (rituals, distance, the "other"), and for offering almost no program for what to do. Fair. But no one names the *affective texture* of digital life — why it feels like freedom while functioning like a trap — better than he does. Use him for the *feeling* and the diagnosis; let your own project supply the missing thing he won't: an actual thing to build.

### Read first
*The Transparency Society* or *Psychopolitics* — each is around 100 small pages and reads quickly. *In the Swarm* for the "no more we" argument. If you read one, read *Psychopolitics* for "smart power" and "freedom as coercion."

### Lines worth quoting
- Smart power "reads and pleases" instead of forbidding; it "adapts itself to the psyche instead of disciplining it."
- "Today we do not deem ourselves subjugated subjects, but rather projects: always refashioning and reinventing ourselves."
- On the digital panopticon: its inmates expose and illuminate *themselves*, voluntarily.
- The "Like" as the digital "Amen"; the smartphone as devotional object.

## Synthesis: how the seven assemble, and where your project breaks the chain

### The argument, in one paragraph
The medium is never neutral: its *form* reshapes us regardless of content (**McLuhan**) and its *design* carries a script that delegates its makers' interests into our behavior, frozen into durable code (**Latour**). That neutrality is an illusion that *conceals* something — the human relations and the extraction behind the commodity (**Marx**), now grown into a whole life lived through representations (**Debord**). And the power doing the concealing has a history: it began as the self-policing visibility of the Panopticon, whose great trick was to run *automatically*, with an empty tower and no nameable owner (**Foucault**); it became continuous, ambient *modulation* that slices us into data-profiles and gates us with codes — the algorithmic feed, described before it existed (**Deleuze**); and it culminated in a "smart power" that doesn't repress but *pleases*, so that we expose and exploit ourselves freely and call it self-expression (**Han**). At every stage the mechanism is the same: **real human control, made to look like a neutral, natural, ownerless thing.**

### Where the ariel app breaks each link
The single-owner app is a counter-move against the *whole* chain, which is why it's more than a gimmick:
- vs. **McLuhan** — it changes the *medium*, not the content, which is the only change McLuhan thinks is real.
- vs. **Latour** — it *opens the black box* and shows the author of the script.
- vs. **Marx** — it *de-fetishizes*: the relation behind the screen is restored to view (it's a relation to a named person).
- vs. **Debord** — it's a *détournement*: it hijacks Instagram's own grammar to critique Instagram.
- vs. **Foucault** — it *re-personalizes* power, collapsing the empty-tower Panopticon back into a visible sovereign.
- vs. **Deleuze** — it makes you an *individual* again, not a dividual; it re-introduces a boundary against endless modulation.
- vs. **Han** — it *re-externalizes* a power that smart power had hidden by internalizing, puncturing the illusion that your activity is purely your own free choice.

### Where the membrane / interception layer breaks each link
- It *re-writes the script* in your favor (**Latour**).
- It reclaims the **code / password** — you decide what is granted and rejected (**Deleuze**).
- It refuses **compulsory transparency**, restoring distance and the right not to be seen (**Han**).
- It is the Situationist **détournement** as working software — the platform's own infrastructure turned against its intended use (**Debord**).

### The one tension that is actually your thesis
**Foucault and Han insist modern power has no single owner — it is automatic, diffuse, and internalized. Your claim is that there *is* an ultimate owner.** These aren't in conflict; the synthesis *is* your essay's central insight: **the big platforms deliberately exploit the appearance of ownerless, automatic, self-chosen power in order to hide the fact that, underneath, someone owns it, decides it, and profits from it.** The ariel app's exaggeration drags that hidden owner back into the light by becoming the *honest* version: a platform that admits it has a king. The discomfort that produces — yes, it's a tiny benevolent dictatorship; yes, it's pure self-exhibition — is not a flaw in the argument. It is the argument.
